The “Christmas Tree” idea: civilizations that don’t line up in time

Overview

Imagine the Galaxy as a dark room. Civilizations are lights that turn on for a while and then go dark. Because their on/off times rarely overlap, the sky looks like a Christmas tree — points flickering here and there, but very few are on together. That makes it hard for neighbours to notice each other.

“Space is big. Time is bigger.”

This page is a visual, intuitive take on one angle of the Fermi paradox: the asynchronous timing of technological civilizations.

Play with the model

Change the parameters and watch the “tree” flicker differently. Shorter lifetimes or fewer civilizations → fewer overlaps.

This is an illustrative toy model, not a prediction.

Core idea (plain English)

  • Each civilization has a start time, a lifetime, and a broadcast habit.
  • Most of the time, two random lifetimes don’t overlap — so mutual detection is rare.
  • Even if they overlap, signals are slow and directional, so it’s easy to miss each other.

Net effect: lots of lights across millions of years, but only a few simultaneously on from any single vantage point.
